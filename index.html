Local Air Quality Management: Disrupting Vehicle Emission Testing Programs

Sponsored by NYU Marron Institute; Conducted by NYU CUSP students

Web Application Code Paper

Project Overview

Salt Lake County oversees a vehicle emissions testing program to ensure compliance with federal air quality standards. Motorists annually spend millions of dollars for inspections carried out under this program.

However, it is not subject to cost controls and suffers from programmatic inefficiencies wherein a large number of compliant vehicles undergo testing and a small number of high-polluting vehicles forgo timely repair. Using survey-based emissions test pricing data, we assess through empirical and spatial autocorrelation analysis the potential economic impact of instituting both emissions test price caps and subsidies to fund compliance repairs.

We conducted two sub-projects: Project A - Economic revision of testing services price schemes; Project B - Modelling of emission test failures.

Introduction

The Salt Lake County vehicle emissions testing program suffers from uncontrolled transactional cost burdens for motorists and is not capable of tracking noncompliant vehicles. How can we leverage Salt Lake County test pricing and vehicle data to reduce cost inefficiencies for motorists and enable tracking capabilities for local government? This project proposes to support Salt Lake County regulators in the exploration and development of new cost efficiencies and noncompliant vehicle tracking capabilities.

We seek to accomplish the following: the identification of testing price points that can accomplish a decrease in cost per ton of reduced pollutant emissions while preserving service availability within the testing provider network, and discernment of patterns in noncompliant vehicle use through ensemble classification methods.

Raw Data

1.  County-wide Testing Facility Price Data, January and February 2019
This dataset contains 435 rows of detail for each test station within Salt Lake County: a unique station ID, test fees, the station name, and street address along with city name and zip codes. The data do not contain point geographies, detail on any additional taxes and fees, and station type categorization info.

2.   Emissions Test Record, 2017-2018, Salt Lake County
The dataset contains independent variables detailing vehicle and testing characteristics and one categorical dependent variable with three values: test pass, test fail, and test rejected due to insufficient data from the vehicle’s onboard computer.

Methodology

1. Stratified sampling of the testing provider population
2. Primary data collection with phone surveys and spatial analyses
3. Local Moran's I map for all test & OBDII test, measuring overall spatial autocorrelation of the dataset
4. Kernel density for station locations, all test counts for each location, and OBDII test counts for each location
5. Geographically Weighted Regression with standard residual map for all test count and OBDII counts
6. Normal spatial distribution including population distribution and spatial joined choropleth map for all test and OBDII test counts

Result

1.    Web Application
A prototype web application displays locations of testing facilities with detailed information on each of them and filter feature based on zip code and price range.

Application will be used by county residents and our intuition. Domain knowledge of our mentor indicated that filtering specifically by zip code will allow users to quickly select the most convenient facilities, while price filter will add transparency to existing pricing schemes and provide a more informed and economically transparent facility selection process.


2.    Local Moran's I
The main outcomes from our analysis include Managers suggested average of 35$ (similar to current county average) as proposed price cap. Around 63% of managers claimed not to offer testing after a price cap is introduced. 60% of managers would be willing to participate in government subsidy programs that would foster timely repairs.


3.    Stats
To support stakeholder decision-making, we have calculated multiple statistics on testing in the county based on the data. We estimated number of initial tests per test result in each year (2017-2018). We calculated the average cost of testing and average price across all tests.

  • Around 7% of initial OBDII tests do not pass, where 4% results are rejects (vehicle not yet ready to take the test due to technical conditions), and 3% are fails (vehicle did not pass the test).
  • Average price of all OBDII tests is currently 33.95$ (to compare, in Cache County, UT price cap is set at 15$, so the difference is significant).Initial results and all statistics have been reported to the stakeholder.

Conclusion

Major risks of the project are associated with communication and delivery. In the research plan we indicate that some steps might require iteration - those include analytical framework development and application development. Therefore, timely delivery will highly depend on successful collaboration with the stakeholder, and among team members, to avoid unnecessary misunderstandings and extensions.

Another risk resides in the team’s capability to deliver high quality products to the end users. Because our stakeholder is a government institution and our work might have sufficient impact on the entire vehicle emission test program, it is crucial to ensure we meet expectations and produce high-end deliverables.

To mitigate this risk we often sought advice from more experienced peers, eg. We have contacted professional UX designer to conduct our application tests. We also tried to quickly identify potentially difficult tasks and issues to address them together and find solutions during team calls or meetings.
